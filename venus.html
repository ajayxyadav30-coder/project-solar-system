VENUS

Venus is the second planet from the Sun and often called Earth's "sister planet" due to their similar size, mass, and bulk composition. However, the similarities end there; Venus is a world of extreme, scorching heat and immense atmospheric pressure. It is classified as a Terrestrial (Rocky) Planet.

üî• The Runaway Greenhouse Effect

Venus is the **hottest planet** in our Solar System, despite not being the closest to the Sun (that's Mercury)5. This extreme temperature is due to a runaway greenhouse effect:

**Dense Atmosphere:** The atmosphere is incredibly dense and heavy, consisting of over **96% carbon dioxide ($\text{CO}_2$)**6, 7.

**Heat Trap:** The dense $\text{CO}_2$8 acts like a thick blanket, trapping solar heat and preventing it from radiating back into space9.

**Uniform Heat:** The planet's slow rotation and dense atmosphere distribute heat very efficiently, meaning the temperature is relatively uniform day and night, equator to pole.

üí® Atmosphere and Weather

The atmosphere of Venus is toxic and crushing10:

**Pressure:** The atmospheric pressure on the surface is about **92 times** that of Earth's sea level, equivalent to the pressure felt about 1 kilometer (0.6 miles) deep in Earth's ocean.

**Sulfuric Acid Clouds:** High-altitude clouds are composed of **sulfuric acid**, creating a corrosive environment11.

**Winds:** While the planet rotates very slowly (a Venusian day is longer than its year), the top layers of the atmosphere race around the planet in only four Earth days, a phenomenon called **super-rotation**12.

The surface of Venus is hidden from view by its permanent cloud cover and has been mapped using radar by missions like NASA's Magellan spacecraft13.

**Volcanic Activity:** The surface features evidence of extensive **volcanic activity**, with vast plains covered in lava flows14. There are two major highland regions: Aphrodite Terra and Ishtar Terra15.

**Retrograde Rotation:** Venus rotates backward (**retrograde**) compared to most other planets in the Solar System16. This means that if you could stand on Venus, the Sun would appear to rise in the west and set in the east. The cause of this unique rotation is still debated, but it may be the result of a massive ancient collision.
